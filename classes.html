CLASSES

INFSCI XXXX: Prototyping Educational Technologies (Fall)

This hands-on lab course invites students to design and build educational technologies from the ground up, using state-of-the-art fabrication tools including 3D printers, laser cutters, sensors, and robotics to prototype learning toolkits, educational toys, science kits, and technology-enhanced manipulatives. Grounded in constructionist learning theory, students develop both the theoretical vocabulary and the practical skills to critique and create meaningful tools for learning. A user-centered design process guides the work throughout, with a special emphasis on low-cost, appropriate technologies for a wide range of learners. No prior programming or technical background is required, and code-free pathways are always available. Students from education, the humanities, art, psychology, and technical fields are all welcome, and all will find a path to making their ideas real, whether the end product is a toy, a tangible interface, a science kit, or a piece of software. This course takes place in the Open Lab (makerspace), Hillman Library. It draws heavily on Bertrand Schneider's Rapid Prototyping of Educational Technologies course at Harvard and Paulo Blikstein's Bits and Atoms course at Teachers College, Columbia University.

INFSCI 0410: Human-Centered Systems (Spring)

This course immerses students in the principles and practice of human-centered interface design, using Design Thinking as a core methodology. Students learn to center the needs of real users at every stage of the design process, moving from early brainstorming and user interviews through low-fidelity sketching, iterative prototyping, and final implementation. Human-Centered Computing (HCC) theory guides students in critically analyzing existing systems and building their own, culminating in a medium-to high-fidelity interface in an application area of their choosing. No programming experience is required, and no backend coding is expected. Students are encouraged to focus on their own learning goals with respect to software, using the tools that serve their project rather than feeling bound to any particular stack. Hands-on tutorials in Figma and React are provided, with an emphasis on developing the independence and problem-solving disposition that characterizes strong designers. This course draws inspiration from James Landay' course at Stanford.

INFSCI 3350: Learning, Technology, and Design (Doctoral Seminar, Fall 2025)

This doctoral seminar provides both theoretical and practical foundations in the learning sciences, preparing students to engage meaningfully with the field as researchers and practitioners. Over the course of the semester, students explore the history and scope of the discipline, examine its core commitments (including the view of humans as sense and meaning makers), and consider what these commitments mean for the design of learning environments. The course also takes up selected forms of learning, such as learning by visualizing, learning through making, and learning with the body, alongside the theories and research methods used to study them. Drawing on design-based research principles and tools such as conjecture mapping, students apply these frameworks to propose a technology-mediated learning environment of their own. Classes center on in-depth discussion of readings in light of students' experiences and reflections, complemented by short lectures, and student presentations.
